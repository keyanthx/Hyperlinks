how to read it

Placeholder text for testing scroll. The hyperlink sits quietly inside a sentence, underlined and blue, waiting. Most of the time we do not think about it: we click and we move on. But the hyperlink is not just a feature of the web — it is the web's most fundamental idea.

Without it, there are only documents, floating separately, unreachable from one another. The link is what makes connection possible: one document pointing to another, across servers, across countries, across languages, without asking anyone's permission.

You do not need to apply to link to something. You do not need to own the content you are pointing to. You just link. That freedom is a radical idea, and it was built into the architecture of the web from the very beginning.

structure

The structure that emerges from billions of these connections is not a library, not a filing cabinet, not a tree with a root and branches — it is something stranger: a network without a centre. A mesh, not a tree.

decay

A broken link is called link rot. Like organic matter, links decay over time. A page moves, a server goes offline, a company shuts down, a URL structure changes — and suddenly a connection that existed becomes a dead end.

The web's memory is shorter than we think. This is partly a technical problem, but it is also a structural one: the hyperlink points outward, but it receives no signal when the thing it points to disappears. There is no notification of death; the connection simply stops working.

platforms

Something changed. The web that exists today is not the web that was designed. For most people, most of the time, the web is three or four platforms — a search engine, a social network, a video service, a marketplace. These platforms are not the web; they are built on top of it.

A platform is, by definition, centralised. All content flows through one company's servers; all connections are mediated by one company's algorithms. The open web of interconnected documents has been replaced, for most users, by a curated stream you scroll through but do not truly navigate.

alternatives

There is still a small but persistent movement of people who maintain personal sites outside of platforms — the indie web, the small web. They use feeds instead of algorithms, link to each other directly, and write for readers rather than for engagement metrics.

It is a minority practice now, but it is a living memory of what the web was once like, and what it could still be: a space of genuine connection, built from individual choices to link.
